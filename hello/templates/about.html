Problem

How can we make academic planning at Princeton a better experience?

We started with this question researching the problem using a design thinking approach. We spoke to numerous stakeholders in the ecosystem from students to advisors to administrators. Time and again we came across automatable, manual labor that was keeping people from engaging in more important aspects of the course planning process. Instead of reflecting on the big picture, students had to search across incomplete platforms to find and meet obscure requirements. Instead of advising, advisors were forced to act as human calculators in the University's checks and balances system. All the while, the administration struggled to keep information current and to present it in a useful and actionable format. The Result: Students were forced to build messy, time-intensive and often incomplete spreadsheets to keep track of their long-term course plans. Certificates posed the most significant issues to this spreadsheet model as there was no way of centralising or parsing the plethora of information online. So, we decided to target this pain point.
